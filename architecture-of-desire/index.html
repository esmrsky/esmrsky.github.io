Part I Empirical

The Foundations of Flourishing

Human needs form an integrated system. Thwart one dimension — biological, psychological, existential — and the whole structure destabilises. We begin with the three “nutrients” of Self-Determination Theory, the most strongly evidenced framework in this synthesis.

The three nutrients

Select a state to see how it reshapes the balance. The shape matters more than the numbers.

Attachment security

Relational co-regulation is the scaffold for the developing brain. Secure attachment creates a base for exploration; insecure styles tend toward hypervigilance or compulsive self-reliance.

The seven affective systems

Panksepp proposed seven primary-process circuits — SEEKING, LUST, CARE, PLAY, FEAR, RAGE and PANIC/GRIEF — conserved across mammals. Note that SEEKING, the appetitive system, is the engine of curiosity. It is not a fault to be corrected.

This taxonomy is influential but not consensus — constructionist accounts reject the premise of discrete, evolutionarily fixed emotion circuits. Treat it as a useful vocabulary rather than settled neuroanatomy.

Self-transcendence

Beyond the three nutrients lies the search for meaning past the ego. Frankl's claim — that meaning is found rather than manufactured — is a philosophical position with clinical support, not an experimental result.

Part II Empirical

Wanting, Liking, Learning

The strongest finding here: wanting something and enjoying it run on different systems, and they can come apart. Berridge and Robinson separate reward into three components that usually travel together — and, under the right conditions, dramatically fail to.

Wanting

Incentive salience. Dopaminergic. Makes a cue feel magnetic and pulls you toward it. Felt as craving, urgency, the tug of the notification.

Liking

Hedonic impact. Opioid and endocannabinoid, in small hotspots. The actual pleasure. Far more spatially limited in the brain than wanting.

Learning

Associative prediction. Binds cues to outcomes so the world becomes navigable. The mechanism by which a place, a time, or a mood becomes a trigger.

The reward dissociation

Repeated exposure can sensitise wanting while hedonic impact stays flat or declines — craving grows as satisfaction shrinks.

Why it escalates

Dopamine tracks prediction error — better than expected — not goodness. As a reward becomes predictable, the signal shrinks even though the reward is unchanged.

This is why escalation is built in rather than a failure of willpower: holding the input constant guarantees the response fades. Novelty, variability and volume are the only ways to restore the signal — which is precisely what engineered feeds supply.
Surface want

The strategy

Concrete tactics for resolving distress. Substitutable, immediately available, and the primary target of attention markets.

“I want a thousand likes on this post.”
Deep need

The requirement

Universal conditions for flourishing. Non-substitutable, slow to satisfy, requiring effort and genuine vulnerability.

“I need to be understood and to matter to someone.”

Desire is not the problem. A life without wanting is not a liberated life, it is a depressed one. The problem is decoupling — when the system that pulls you toward something stops being informed by the system that registers whether it was any good.

Part III Empirical

The Anatomy of Enjoyment

It is easy to catalogue what goes wrong and much harder to say what enjoyment positively is. This part does the harder thing: the conditions that reliably produce absorption, the distinct kinds of fun, and the learnable skill of getting more from what you already have.

The flow channel

Absorption happens where challenge and skill are both high and matched. Click or drag anywhere on the map.

Flow

What flow requires

Clear goals, immediate feedback, and a challenge that stretches without exceeding you. Self-consciousness recedes, time distorts, and the activity becomes worth doing for its own sake — what Csikszentmihalyi called autotelic.

The effort paradox

We assume effort is a cost to minimise. But effort frequently adds value to its outcome — people prefer what they worked for, and difficulty is much of why games are fun. An experience engineered to demand nothing has removed the main ingredient.

Anticipation is real pleasure

Looking forward to something is not merely instrumental — it is a substantial part of the enjoyment, and sometimes exceeds the event. Removing all delay from a pleasure often removes most of it. Friction is not always the enemy.

Experiencing vs. remembering

Kahneman distinguished the self that lives an experience from the self that recalls it — and they disagree constantly. Memory weights peaks and endings and ignores duration, so what you choose to repeat rests on a distorted record.

Four kinds of fun

Caillois argued that play is not one thing. Most people reliably prefer some of these over others — and knowing which kind you were actually after explains a great deal about why a given “fun” activity left you flat.

The second axis: free play to formal rules Caillois placed every kind of fun on a spectrum from paidia — improvised, turbulent, rule-free — to ludus, where arbitrary constraints are deliberately added to make an activity harder and therefore better. Both ends are legitimate. Trouble finding enjoyment is often a mismatch on this axis rather than a deficit of desire.
Paidia — free
Ludus — structured

Savouring: enjoyment as a skill

The capacity to extract enjoyment from a good experience varies between people and can be trained. Most of us under-consume the pleasures we already have while pursuing new ones.

Part IV Interpretive

Purpose and Performance

What play is to leisure, vocation is to work — and it fails the same way. Byung-Chul Han argues that late modernity replaced the external overseer with an internal one, so the subject now exploits itself and calls the result freedom. This is social criticism, not measurement: a lens, offered as a lens.

Restorative play vs. empty stimulation

The difference is not what the activity looks like from outside — it is who authored the experience, and whether you had real agency inside it.

The magic circle

Rules entered voluntarily, genuine agency within them, absorption in flow. Huizinga's term for the bounded space where play becomes its own world.

AgencyAbsorptionRejuvenation

Attentional capture

Variable rewards on someone else's schedule, consumption without authorship. The form resembles play; the agency that made play restorative is missing.

Scheduled rewardNo authorshipEmptiness

Purpose vs. performance

In late modernity vocation is quietly replaced by performance — the internalised demand for perpetual self-optimisation.

The order of love

Work as relational service and gift. Worth is received rather than won, so effort carries no existential threat.

CaritasGiftEnduring peace

Entrepreneur of the self

Worth must be continuously re-earned through output. The subject becomes both exploiter and exploited — a war with no external enemy to resist.

Part VIII Confessional

The New Covenant Answer

This part changes register deliberately, and you should know that before reading it. Everything above describes a problem it cannot solve: the wanting system cannot be argued into wanting differently, and rules imposed from outside do not reach it. What follows is the Christian claim that this is exactly the problem the New Covenant addresses — not by suppressing desire, but by giving it a new object and a new heart.

It is offered as a confession rather than a conclusion. The preceding seven parts do not entail it.
