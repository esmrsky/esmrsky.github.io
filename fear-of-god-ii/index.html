The Fear of God

Sixteen questions, honest answers first. We talk before we tap — the reveals hold scripture, the original languages, and places the conversation can go.

HOW THIS WORKS — Read the question. Sit in the silence. Say the honest thing, not the church one. Then reveal. Translations switch any time with the pills above.

01Open floor

What comes to mind when you hear "the fear of God"?

Not the right answer — the honest one. First word, first image, first flinch.

Discuss
Terror — being scared of punishment, hell, lightning bolts
Respect — "it just means respect" (the quick defuse)
Distance — God as unapproachable, keep your head down
Awe — bigness, smallness, standing at the edge of something vast
Old religion — "that's an Old Testament thing"
Nothing — some have honestly never thought about it

No wrong answers here — every one of these is a thread the rest of the night pulls on. If "it just means respect" is in the room, hold it loosely: question 05 tests whether the original words let us off that easily.

02The feeling itself

How would you describe the feeling of fear?

Any fear. What does it do to your body, your thoughts, your attention?

Discuss

Heart pounding. Time slowing. Tunnel vision. Going quiet. And underneath all of it, one thing: you can't think about anything else.

Fear is the body's involuntary attention system. Whatever you fear owns your focus completely — your posture orients toward it, your breathing changes, everything else in the room goes dim. You cannot casually fear something. Fear is total.

Hold onto that. It becomes the hinge of the whole night at question 07: if fear is involuntary total attention, what would it mean to give that same totality to God — on purpose?

03Into the text

Does fear split into good fear and bad fear?

And if so — where's the line?

Exodus 20:20NIV

Moses said to the people, "Do not be afraid. God has come to test you, so that the fear of God will be with you to keep you from sinning."

"Don't be afraid," Moses answered them, "for God has come in this way to test you, and so that your fear of him will keep you from sinning!"

Sinai. Thunder, smoke, the mountain trembling. The people are terrified — and Moses says both things in one breath.
Text

"Do not fear... so that the fear of God will be with you." One verse, both fears — and in Hebrew it's the same root word both times (yare). So the split isn't between two different words. It's between two directions the same capacity can point.

Discuss

One fear makes Israel back away from the mountain. The other is meant to stay with them and keep them from sin when the smoke is gone. So — what's the difference between fear that drives you from God and fear that holds you near him?

Scripture's claims are strange if fear only means dread: it's the starting point of wisdom, it's teachable, it leads to life and rest, and it's clean. Nobody describes panic that way. Whatever this is, the Bible treats it as a treasure, not a threat.

05The original words

Many words in the original — all translated "fear"?

English flattens several Hebrew and Greek words into one. What are we actually reading?

Text
יִרְאָה
yirah · verb yare
The workhorse — by far the most common. Spans terror, reverence, and awe depending on object and context. Both halves of Exodus 20:20 use this root. So does nora — "awesome" (Ps 47:2): every time we sing that God is awesome, we're singing the fear-word.
פַּחַד
pachad
Dread, trembling terror. Strong enough that "the Fear of Isaac" is used as a name of God himself (Gen 31:42) — the One before whom Isaac trembled.
אֵימָה
emah
Terror that falls on people — "terror and dread will fall on them" (Ex 15:16). Overwhelming, external, event-like.
מוֹרָא
morah
Fear owed — reverence, awe. "If I am a master, where is the respect due me?" (Mal 1:6 — NIV renders morah as "respect"). Isa 8:13 makes it personal: let the LORD himself be your fear.
חָרַד
charad
To tremble, quake. The person God esteems is the one "who trembles at my word" (Isa 66:2). Trembling as teachability — the opposite of a calloused ear.
עָרַץ
aratz
To dread, stand in terror. Scripture aims it both ways: "do not be afraid" of men (Josh 1:9), but of God — "he is the one you are to dread" (Isa 8:13). Not less fear; relocated fear.

Six words where English has one — and translators still make choices inside the range. Watch Psalm 19:9 across the pills above: NIV says "fear," NLT says reverence — same Hebrew word, two English readings of where it sits. And Isa 8:13 rewards reading in full: "do not fear what they fear" — the fear of the Lord isn't added to your fears; it replaces them.

Text
φόβος
phobos
The broad Greek term — root of "phobia," but in the NT it covers everything from panic to holy reverence. It's the word in Acts 9:31 and Phil 2:12.
δέος
deos
Awe. Appears once in the NT — Heb 12:28, paired with eulabeia: "with reverence and awe." The trembling edge of new-covenant worship.
εὐλάβεια
eulabeia
Careful, devoted reverence. Heb 12:28 pairs it with deos. And Heb 5:7 says Jesus' own prayers were heard because of this very thing.
τρόμος
tromos
Trembling — the body agreeing with the moment. "Fear and trembling" (Phil 2:12) isn't panic; it's the shake of hands holding something weighty.
ἔκφοβος
ekphobos
Utterly terrified. Heb 12:21 — even Moses at Sinai: "I am trembling with fear." The old mountain's word, kept in the past tense of the story.
δειλία
deilia
Cowardice, timidity. Used once — 2 Tim 1:7 — and never for the fear of God. This is the fear God does not give.
εὐσέβεια
eusebeia · sebomai
Godliness, devotion — from sebomai, "to revere, to worship." Luke calls Gentile seekers both "worshipers" (sebomenoi, Acts 13:43) and "God-fearing" (Acts 10:2) — for him the two labels name one posture.
προσκυνέω
proskyneo
Not a fear-word at all: to bow toward, to kiss toward — whole-body homage. Remember this one. It's about to matter.
2 Timothy 1:7NIV

For the Spirit God gave us does not make us timid, but gives us power, love and self-discipline.

For God has not given us a spirit of fear and timidity, but of power, love, and self-discipline.

For God has not given us a spirit of timidity, but of power and love and discipline.

For God will never give you the spirit of fear, but the Holy Spirit who gives you mighty power, love, and self-control.

The TPT footnote on this verse is worth reading aloud: the "fear" here is fear of man — and "the fear of God prevents us from fearing others." One fear displaces all the rest.
06Jesus

What does Jesus say about it?

He talks about fear more than we remember — and he says two things that seem to collide.

Luke 12:4–5NIV

"I tell you, my friends, do not be afraid of those who kill the body and after that can do nothing more. But I will show you whom you should fear: Fear him who, after your body has been killed, has authority to throw you into hell. Yes, I tell you, fear him."

"Dear friends, don't be afraid of those who want to kill your body; they cannot do any more to you after that. But I'll tell you whom to fear. Fear God, who has the power to kill you and then throw you into hell. Yes, he's the one to fear."

"I say to you, My friends, do not be afraid of those who kill the body, and after that have nothing more that they can do. But I will warn you whom to fear: fear the One who, after He has killed, has authority to throw you into hell; yes, I tell you, fear Him!"

"Listen, my beloved friends, don't fear those who may want to take your life but can do nothing more. It's true that they may kill your body, but they have no power over your soul. The one you must fear is God, for he has both the power to take your life and the authority to cast your soul into hell. Yes, the only one you need to fear is God."

Notice he says it to "my friends." This is not a threat to enemies — it's counsel to people he loves.
Luke 12:32 — twenty-seven verses laterNIV

"Do not be afraid, little flock, for your Father has been pleased to give you the kingdom."

"So don't be afraid, little flock. For it gives your Father great happiness to give you the Kingdom."

"Do not be afraid, little flock, for your Father has chosen gladly to give you the kingdom."

"So don't ever be afraid, dearest friends! Your loving Father joyously gives you his kingdom with all its promises!"

Same chapter. Same speaker. Same audience.
Text

In one conversation Jesus says "fear him" and "do not be afraid." In Matthew's version (Matt 10:28–31) they're four verses apart: fear the One who can destroy soul and body — and don't be afraid, you are worth more than many sparrows. He clearly doesn't think these cancel each other out.

Discuss

How can both be true at once? What kind of fear survives being told "your Father joyously gives you the kingdom" — and what kind doesn't?

07The word swap

Why does Jesus quote "fear the Lord" — and say "worship the Lord"?

In the wilderness, Jesus quotes Deuteronomy 6:13 back at Satan. But one word comes out different.

TPT — this book isn't yet published in The Passion Translation. Tap another translation above.

Look at the NASB. Even in Deuteronomy itself, the translators render "serve him" (avad) as "worship Him." The overlap Jesus exploits is already visible to translators.
Matthew 4:10NIV

Jesus said to him, "Away from me, Satan! For it is written: 'Worship the Lord your God, and serve him only.'"

"Get out of here, Satan," Jesus told him. "For the Scriptures say, 'You must worship the LORD your God and serve only him.'"

Then Jesus said to him, "Go away, Satan! For it is written, 'You shall worship the Lord your God, and serve Him only.'"

But Jesus said, "Go away, Satan! For the Scriptures say: Kneel before the Lord your God and worship only him."

One verse earlier, Satan had demanded exactly this word: "if you will bow down and worship (proskyneo) me." Jesus answers the worship-demand with the fear-command — rendered as worship.
Scholarship

Careful here — "intentionally misquotes" is stronger than we can prove. The Hebrew of Deuteronomy 6:13 says fear (yare). Most copies of the Greek Old Testament (the Septuagint) also say fear — but some manuscripts read worship (proskyneseis), matching Jesus' wording. So either Jesus is deliberately rendering fear as worship, or he's using a Greek rendering that had already made that move before him.

Either way, the equivalence stands as legitimate. In the mouth of Jesus, "fear the Lord" and "worship the Lord" occupy the same slot — and the context sharpens it, because the whole scene is a fight over who gets his worship.

Interpretation

Now bring back question 02. Fear is involuntary total attention — the threat owns your focus, your posture, your breathing. Proskyneo is a posture word: to bow toward, to orient your whole body at something. Put them together and a working definition appears:

Fear of the Lord is the totality of fear, aimed at worth instead of threat — undivided attention, given on purpose. That's a framework to test tonight, not a dictionary entry. See if the verses can carry it.

Discuss

Run the substitution through the verses we've already read and see what happens:

"The worship of the LORD is the beginning of wisdom" — Prov 9:10 (TPT already prints it this way)
"Worship the LORD with reverence and rejoice with trembling" — Ps 2:11 (NASB already prints it this way)
"Give me an undivided heart, that I may attend to your name" — Ps 86:11
"The church... living in the worship of the Lord... increased in numbers" — Acts 9:31

Where does the substitution illuminate, and where does it lose something? (It shouldn't work everywhere — pachad-dread is real too. Find the edges together.)

08Covenants

Old covenant, new covenant — did the fear of God change?

"Perfect love casts out fear." So is fear of God over?

Text · Hebrews 12:18–29

Sinai — where you have not come

  • Fire, darkness, gloom, storm
  • A boundary — touch the mountain and die
  • Even Moses: "I am trembling with fear"
  • The terror of distance

Zion — where you have come

  • The city of the living God, angels in joyful assembly
  • Jesus, mediator of a new covenant
  • Yet still: "worship God with reverence and awe"
  • The awe of access — "for our God is a consuming fire"

The covenant changed. The fire didn't. What changed is where you stand in relation to it.

Therefore, since we receive a kingdom which cannot be shaken, let us show gratitude, by which we may offer to God an acceptable service with reverence and awe; for our God is a consuming fire.

Since we are receiving our rights to an unshakable kingdom we should be extremely thankful and offer God the purest worship that delights his heart as we lay down our lives in absolute surrender, filled with awe. For our God is a holy, devouring fire!

The new-covenant conclusion is not "so relax" — it's reverence and awe, on the far side of grace.

Read all three. The new covenant doesn't delete the fear of God — it promises to plant it in our hearts. And watch what the NLT does with the word.
1 John 4:18NIV

There is no fear in love. But perfect love drives out fear, because fear has to do with punishment. The one who fears is not made perfect in love.

Such love has no fear, because perfect love expels all fear. If we are afraid, it is for fear of punishment, and this shows that we have not fully experienced his perfect love.

There is no fear in love; but perfect love casts out fear, because fear involves punishment, and the one who fears is not perfected in love.

Love never brings fear, for fear is always related to punishment. But love's perfection drives the fear of punishment far from our hearts. Whoever walks constantly afraid of punishment has not reached love's perfection.

John tells us exactly which fear gets evicted: the one tied to punishment. TPT's footnote agrees — fear of correction, punishment, rejection. Awe was never on the eviction notice.
Psalm 130:4NIV

But with you there is forgiveness, so that we can, with reverence, serve you.

But you offer forgiveness, that we might learn to fear you.

But there is forgiveness with You, that You may be feared.

But your forgiving love is what makes you so wonderful. No wonder you are loved and worshiped!

The most backwards verse in the Bible, until it isn't: forgiveness produces fear. Dread ends where forgiveness begins — and awe starts there. And watch TPT render "feared" as loved and worshiped — the swap, again.
Scholarship

The historic answer is that two different fears are wearing one English word. Augustine and Aquinas named them, and the distinction has carried ever since:

Servile fear — cast out

  • Dreads punishment — "he's going to get me"
  • Motive: self-preservation
  • Hides, tiptoes, manages its image
  • Hates sin for what it costs me
  • Shrinks as love is experienced (1 Jn 4:18)

Filial fear — planted

  • Dreads grieving a Father — "he's this good?"
  • Motive: love and awe of who he is
  • Confesses, comes close, stays
  • Hates sin for what it does to him
  • Grows as love is experienced (Jer 32:40)
Interpretation

That's the reconciliation. Perfect love casts out the servile fear — the terror of what God will do to you. The new covenant plants the filial fear — the awe of who he is. Augustine's sharp observation: a person who avoids sin only out of servile fear hasn't beaten sin at all — their heart still loves it, and only the threat is holding them back.

Ps 130:4 is the hinge where they trade places: dread ends where forgiveness begins, and awe starts there. Grace doesn't shrink God. It brings you close enough to finally see how big he is.

09In the body

Two fears, two nervous systems — what does each one do to you?

Back to question 02. If fear is total attention, the two fears shouldn't just feel different. They should run on different machinery.

Scholarship

Researchers who study awe describe it with two ingredients: perceived vastness — something bigger than you — and the need to accommodate, meaning your mental model has to resize to fit what you just met.

Here's the fork. Vastness alone doesn't decide anything. The same overwhelming thing can be appraised two ways, and the body goes two completely different directions:

Appraised as threat

  • Fight-or-flight surges — heart rate, cortisol
  • The body's alarm system stays lit
  • Thinking goes rigid — hyper-vigilant, defensive
  • Behavior: avoid, flee, hide
  • Chronically: exhaustion, shutdown

Appraised as safe vastness

  • The calm-and-connect branch engages
  • Self-focused chatter quiets — the "small self"
  • Attention turns outward; thinking stays flexible
  • Behavior: approach, open up, give
  • Downstream: compassion, generosity

Same vastness. Opposite outcome. The whole difference is the appraisal: is this bigness coming for me, or holding me? Awe research even has a name for the first one — the threat-based variant, the dark side of the sublime.

And this tracks with how people attach. Someone whose deep model of God is safe approaches him when things go wrong. Someone whose model is punitive braces and withdraws — from the same God, reading the same bigness.

Interpretation

Exodus 20:20 is the fork, described three thousand years early. One mountain. One vastness. Israel backs away — and Moses says the fear that stays with you is a different thing entirely. Same stimulus, two appraisals, two directions. The text already knew.

Servile and filial aren't just two attitudes — they're two states. Dread runs on the alarm system; awe runs on the calm-and-connect system. Which is why "just fear God more" is useless advice to someone stuck in dread. You can't get to filial fear by adding servile fear. Different machinery.

Awe's definition is the wisdom thread. Vastness plus accommodation — seeing something at true size and letting your model resize to fit it. That is almost word for word what we said wisdom is (Prov 9:10). The starting point of wisdom is the one moment you stop shrinking God to a manageable size.

The "small self" is the Isaiah 6 arc. Awe quiets self-preoccupation — and Isaiah, undone in front of the throne, ends up volunteering. Not collapse. Unselfing. The self gets quiet enough to hear "whom shall I send?"

And Eden. The first recorded response to God's approach was hiding. That's the threat appraisal, in the very first chapter of the story.

Scholarship — confidence check

Reasonably solid: awe involves vastness plus mental accommodation; awe reduces self-referential focus and increases prosocial behavior; threat and safety are genuinely different autonomic states; a threat-based variant of awe exists and behaves differently from the positive kind.

Suggestive, not settled: specific claims about brain structure — that a punitive God-image correlates with measurable volume differences — come from small studies, one of the best-known with about forty participants. The direction is interesting. It is not "science proves your brain shrinks." Don't preach it.

Discarded: the old "God spot" idea — one dedicated religion module in the temporal lobe. The field abandoned it for distributed networks. If you've heard it, it's out of date.

The guardrail

Worth saying plainly: none of this proves anything about God. A scan can describe the state; it can't validate the object. It can't tell you your awe is aimed at something real, or aimed rightly — idols produce awe too, and so do stadiums. The mechanism is not the message.

What it can do is confirm that the two fears scripture distinguishes are not a wordplay. They're visible all the way down to your heart rate. The text drew the line first; the body just keeps agreeing with it.

10The displacement

When you fear God, do the other fears just fall off?

Or is that a nicer way of saying distract yourself?

Psalm 112:1, 7–8NIV

Praise the LORD. Blessed are those who fear the LORD, who find great delight in his commands… They will have no fear of bad news; their hearts are steadfast, trusting in the LORD. Their hearts are secure, they will have no fear; in the end they will look in triumph on their foes.

Praise the LORD! How joyful are those who fear the LORD and delight in obeying his commands… They do not fear bad news; they confidently trust the LORD to care for them. They are confident and fearless and can face their foes triumphantly.

Praise the LORD! How blessed is the man who fears the LORD, who greatly delights in His commandments… He will not fear evil tidings; his heart is steadfast, trusting in the LORD. His heart is upheld, he will not fear, until he looks with satisfaction on his adversaries.

Shout in celebration of praise to the Lord! Everyone who loves the Lord and delights in him will cherish his words and be blessed beyond expectation… They will not live in fear or dread of what may come, for their hearts are firm, ever secure in their faith. Steady and strong, they will not be afraid, but will calmly face their every foe until they all go down in defeat.

The whole psalm is one long equation: fears the LORD in verse 1 → no fear of bad news in verse 7. And read TPT closely — it renders the fear-word as "loves the Lord and delights in him." The swap, one more time.
Proverbs 29:25NIV

Fear of man will prove to be a snare, but whoever trusts in the LORD is kept safe.

Fearing people is a dangerous trap, but trusting the LORD means safety.

The fear of man brings a snare, but he who trusts in the LORD will be exalted.

Fear and intimidation is a trap that holds you back. But when you place your confidence in the Lord, you will be seated in the high place.

The Hebrew here for "fear of man" is cherdah — trembling — from the same root as Isaiah 66:2, where the one who trembles at God's word is the one he looks on with favor. Identical trembling. One is a snare; the other is what God delights in. It was never the trembling that differed. Only the object.
Isaiah 8:13 — "Do not fear what they fear… he is the one you are to fear" — relocation, in one command
Psalm 27:1 — "The LORD is my light and my salvation — whom shall I fear?" — a question, not a technique
Psalm 34:4 — "he delivered me from all my fears" — all of them, by one encounter
2 Timothy 1:7 — the Spirit doesn't give timidity; the fear of God is what evicts the fear of man
Discuss

The tempting explanation: attention is finite. God is huge. He fills the tank, and the small anxieties get no bandwidth. Crowded out.

It's intuitive, and it's the weakest version of a strong idea. Push on it with the texts we just read:

Prov 19:23 — "rests content, untouched by trouble." Untouched by it. The trouble is still there.
Ps 112:7 — "no fear of bad news." The news arrives. He hears it. His heart is steady while receiving it.
Luke 12 — Jesus gives a reason, not a distraction: "they can do nothing more"; "you're worth more than sparrows."
Daniel 3 — "even if he does not." They can see the furnace perfectly well. They've ranked it.

And the fatal one: bandwidth is fragile. Distraction lasts exactly as long as the focus does. Look away and it all floods back — that's spiritual whack-a-mole. But Jer 32:40 promises permanence: "so that they will never turn away." No capacity trick delivers that.

So the other fears are not erased. Something else happens to them.

Interpretation

Start here: all fear is a power-assessment. You fear something because you believe it can take something you love. Fear of man = their opinion can take my worth. Fear of lack = money can take my security. Fear of death = death can take my life. Name the fear and you've named what you think has power over you.

1 · Jurisdiction — and this is Jesus' own argument. Fear of the Lord is recognizing that God holds all of it. Then comes the turn: that God has bound himself to you in covenant love. Every other power is demoted, not deleted. They can still act — they cannot reach what he's holding. "They can kill the body, and after that can do nothing more" isn't they aren't frightening. It's their authority has a ceiling, and his doesn't.

2 · Rescaling. Awe forces your model to resize (question 09). Once God is at true size in your map, everything else is permanently smaller relative to him — not because you're staring at him, but because the map itself changed. That's why it survives you looking away.

3 · The brake. Here's the bandwidth intuition, corrected. It isn't that there's no room. It's that the calm-and-connect state and the alarm state can't run at the same time. Filial awe puts your body in a state the other fears need you not to be in. Not a full hard drive — an active brake.

The line

The fear of the Lord is the only fear that makes you fearless. Every other fear multiplies — fear of man breeds more fear of man, anxiety compounds into anxiety. This is the only one that ends the chain. Why? Because it's the only fear whose object loves you.

Which is Luke 12 in a single breath: fear him — and don't be afraid, little flock. Same mouth. Same conversation. Same person. There was never a contradiction to fix.

Say this plainly

This is not a technique, and it is not advice for an anxious person. "Just fear God more" is useless to someone already living in dread — you'd be stacking fear on fear, not displacing it. You cannot get to filial fear by adding servile fear. Different machinery (question 08).

Displacement is a byproduct of seeing, not a lever you pull. Nobody in scripture achieves it. Isaiah gets shown a throne. Peter gets a boat full of fish. The disciples get told what they're worth. In every case the fear falls off downstream of a revelation — it's never the assignment.

And "no fear of bad news" is not numbness. Ps 112 says the heart is steady, not that the heart stops feeling. Jesus wept. Paul had sleepless nights. The fear of the Lord doesn't delete grief, caution, medical care, or wisdom about real danger — those aren't the fears in question. What it ends is the fear that rules.

Discuss

So: name one fear you're actually carrying. Not a noble one — a real one. Now ask the jurisdiction question about it: what exactly do I believe this thing can take from me — and does it actually have the authority to take it?

09The church now

What happened to the church? Is the fear of God a fading subject?

When did you last hear it preached? And the motives we do have — "I don't want to hurt him," "I don't want to disappoint him," "He is so holy and I am such a sinner" — are those the fear of the Lord?

Discuss

The honest diagnosis first: have we traded awe for familiarity? Is "God is my friend" the whole gospel we tell? Then test the motives on the table:

"I don't want God to punish me" — 1 John 4:18 says perfect love drives exactly this out. If it's still running the relationship, the problem isn't too much fear; it's not enough experienced love.

"I don't want to hurt him / disappoint him" — this is a different animal. It's not dread of what he'll do to you; it's grief at what you'd do to him. A child who fears the belt and a child who can't bear to break their father's heart are both called "afraid" — but only one of those fears gets cast out. The second one looks a lot like the fear that remains.

The caution: even "I don't want to disappoint him" can quietly turn back into performance — earning, tiptoeing, image-managing. The test is where it drives you: toward him (confession, honesty, nearness) or away from him (hiding). Hiding is Eden's fear. Nearness is Sinai's gift.

Discuss

It isn't as fading as it looks — the subject has been pushed back onto the table from several directions at once, and the people doing the pushing don't agree with each other. A few examples of the range, not endorsements:

John Bevere — argues the church has "villainized" godly fear, and that awe is the missing piece
Paul Ellis — grace-first: biblical fear is breathless adoration, and only the eternally secure can actually tremble well
Dan Mohler — reverence for what the cross cost; refusing to live casually, without becoming sin-conscious
Todd White — publicly repented of a man-centered gospel after an encounter with God's holiness
African Apostolic churches (Zimbabwe) — fear of God as visible, communal, ethical: recited commandments, ordered lives
Augustine · Aquinas — the servile/filial distinction the whole conversation still runs on

Notice the spread: some reach for fear to correct cheap grace, others reach for grace to correct cheap fear. Both corrections exist because both errors are real. Where does our church actually sit on that line — and which error are we more likely to make?

Text

Isaiah 6. The throne, the seraphim, the shaking doorposts. Isaiah's response is exactly "He is so holy and I am such a sinner" — "Woe to me! I am ruined!" But the scene doesn't end there. A coal touches his lips: "your guilt is taken away." And the undone man becomes the volunteering man: "Here am I. Send me."

Luke 5. Peter, after the catch of fish: "Go away from me, Lord; I am a sinful man." Jesus: "Don't be afraid; from now on you will catch people."

Same arc both times: holiness produces the collapse, grace produces the commission. "I am such a sinner" is a doorway in the fear of the Lord — never the room you live in. Awe that ends in groveling is incomplete. Biblical awe ends in sending.

10Practice

What does it look like today? How do I practically live it?

Psalm 86:11NIV

Teach me your way, LORD, that I may rely on your faithfulness; give me an undivided heart, that I may fear your name.

Teach me your ways, O LORD, that I may live according to your truth! Grant me purity of heart, so that I may honor you.

Teach me Your way, O LORD; I will walk in Your truth; unite my heart to fear Your name.

Teach me more about you, how you work and how you move, so that I can walk onward in your truth until everything within me brings honor to your name.

"Unite my heart to fear your name" (NASB) — and TPT's "everything within me" carries the same undivided idea. If fear is undivided attention, David is praying for exactly that. It's asked for, and it's taught (Ps 34:11).
Prov 8:13 — "To fear the LORD is to hate evil" — it's measurable in ethics, not goosebumps
2 Cor 7:1 — "perfecting holiness out of reverence for God"
Interpretation

Put your body in it. Proskyneo is postural — kneel when you pray, stand when the Word is read, lift hands. The body teaches the heart what the mind forgets.

Read the throne room aloud. Isaiah 6, Revelation 1, Revelation 4 — slowly, out loud, until the furniture of heaven stops being familiar. Awe can be rehearsed.

Obey in the small hidden thing this week. Proverbs 8:13 makes fear of the Lord an ethics word: what you do when no one is watching is your actual measurement of it.

Keep short accounts. Confession isn't groveling — it's refusing to hide. Hiding is the wrong fear; nearness is the right one.

Let it eat your other fears. The practical payoff of 2 Timothy 1:7 — the person who kneels lowest before God stands tallest before everything else. Fear of man shrinks in exact proportion to fear of God.

11New Testament

What does the New Testament actually teach on it?

If it were only an old-covenant theme, the epistles would go quiet. Do they?

Acts 9:31NIV

Then the church throughout Judea, Galilee and Samaria enjoyed a time of peace and was strengthened. Living in the fear of the Lord and encouraged by the Holy Spirit, it increased in numbers.

The church then had peace throughout Judea, Galilee, and Samaria, and it became stronger as the believers lived in the fear of the Lord. And with the encouragement of the Holy Spirit, it also grew in numbers.

So the church throughout all Judea and Galilee and Samaria enjoyed peace, being built up; and going on in the fear of the Lord and in the comfort of the Holy Spirit, it continued to increase.

After this, the church all over Judea, Galilee, and Samaria experienced a season of peace. The congregations grew larger and larger, with the believers being empowered and encouraged by the Holy Spirit. They worshiped God in wonder and awe, and walked in the fear of the Lord.

Fear of the Lord + comfort of the Spirit, in the same church, at the same time — and the result is growth. Awe is not a repellent. It's soil. TPT makes the pairing explicit: "worshiped God in wonder and awe, and walked in the fear of the Lord."
Philippians 2:12–13NIV

...continue to work out your salvation with fear and trembling, for it is God who works in you to will and to act in order to fulfill his good purpose.

Work hard to show the results of your salvation, obeying God with deep reverence and fear. For God is working in you, giving you the desire and the power to do what pleases him.

...work out your salvation with fear and trembling; for it is God who is at work in you, both to will and to work for His good pleasure.

Now you must continue to make this new life fully manifested as you live in the holy awe of God—which brings you trembling into his presence. God will continually revitalize you, implanting within you the passion to do what pleases him.

"Fear and trembling" — because God himself is at work in you. The trembling of a surgeon's hands would be fear; this is the trembling of the operating table realizing who's operating.
1 Peter 1:17NIV

Since you call on a Father who judges each person's work impartially, live out your time as foreigners here in reverent fear.

And remember that the heavenly Father to whom you pray has no favorites. He will judge or reward you according to what you do. So you must live in reverent fear of him during your time here as "temporary residents."

If you address as Father the One who impartially judges according to each one's work, conduct yourselves in fear during the time of your stay on earth.

Since you call on him as your heavenly Father, the impartial Judge who judges according to each one's works, live each day with holy awe and reverence throughout your time on earth.

Peter holds "Father" and "fear" in one sentence without blinking. So did Jesus in Luke 12.

Add Hebrews 12:28 (question 08) and 2 Corinthians 7:1, and the pattern is unmistakable: the New Testament doesn't retire the fear of the Lord. It relocates it — from the foot of a forbidden mountain to the inside of a family.

12Tone & fruit

Somber and serious — or joyful? And which fruit of the Spirit does it align with?

Psalm 2:11NIV

Isaiah 11:2–3 — the Messiah himselfNIV

The Spirit of the LORD will rest on him — the Spirit of wisdom and of understanding, the Spirit of counsel and of might, the Spirit of the knowledge and fear of the LORD — and he will delight in the fear of the LORD.

The Spirit of the LORD will rest on Him, the spirit of wisdom and understanding, the spirit of counsel and strength, the spirit of knowledge and the fear of the LORD. And He will delight in the fear of the LORD.

The Spirit of YAHWEH will rest upon him, the Spirit of Extraordinary Wisdom, the Spirit of Perfect Understanding, the Spirit of Wise Strategy, the Spirit of Mighty Power, the Spirit of Revelation, and the Spirit of the Fear of YAHWEH. He will find his delight in living by the Spirit of the Fear of the Lord.

Jesus delights in it. The Hebrew behind "delight" (vaharicho) comes from the root "to smell, to breathe in" — as if the fear of the LORD is fragrance to him. The air the Messiah breathes.
Text

Honest answer first: the fear of the Lord is not one of the nine in Galatians 5:22–23 — "love, joy, peace, forbearance, kindness, goodness, faithfulness, gentleness and self-control." But Isaiah 11:2 lists it among the sevenfold Spirit resting on the Messiah. So it isn't a fruit; it's more like climate — the atmosphere the Spirit's fruit grows in.

Discuss

Which fruit does it feed most directly? Make the case from tonight's verses: Joy (Ps 2:11 — rejoice with trembling; Isa 11:3 — he delights in it). Peace (Prov 19:23 — rests content, untouched by trouble; Acts 9:31 — the fearing church enjoyed peace). Faithfulness (Jer 32:40 — so that they never turn away). Self-control (Prov 8:13 — to fear the LORD is to hate evil; Ex 20:20 — to keep you from sinning).

Interpretation

And notice how the fruit list reads next to question 09. Peace and patience are what the calm-and-connect system produces — the body's own recovery from alarm. Self-control is the executive brain overriding a survival reflex. Love requires enough safety to stop defending yourself. Paul's list isn't a mood board; it describes a person whose alarm is off.

Which is exactly why fear of the Lord functions as climate rather than fruit. Servile dread keeps the alarm lit, and almost nothing on that list grows in an alarm state. Filial awe is the safety the fruit needs to grow in. The atmosphere isn't the harvest — it's what makes a harvest possible.

13The views

Six ways people hold it — which is closest to the texts?

Most of us walked in carrying one of the first five. Weigh each against tonight's verses.

Discuss
"It just means respect."
Gets right: fear isn't panic. Misses: respect doesn't tremble. Sinai shook, Isaiah collapsed, John fell as though dead (Revelation 1). The word is bigger than manners.
"It's terror of punishment."
Gets right: holiness is not casual. Misses: the gospel — punishment-fear is exactly the fear perfect love evicts (1 Jn 4:18). This view keeps people hiding.
"That's an Old Testament thing."
Gets right: something really did change at the cross. Misses: the epistles — the fearing church grows (Acts 9:31), worship still comes with reverence and awe (Heb 12:28), and the new covenant plants the fear of God in hearts (Jer 32:40).
"It's reverent awe."
Gets right: the closest of the four to the texts. The risk: staying abstract — awe as vocabulary we nod at instead of attention we actually give.
"It's obeying the rules."
Gets right: it's measurable — Prov 8:13 makes fear of the LORD an ethics word, and Ex 20:20 ties it to not sinning. Misses: the order. Obedience is what filial fear produces, not what manufactures it. Reverse it and you get performance with God's name on it.
Scholarship

The classic grid sorts it by what you're actually afraid of. Two of these are aimed at God, two at consequences:

Servile
timor servilis
Fears punishment. Motive: self-preservation. Status: cast out by perfect love. Ends in scrupulosity or legalism.
Filial
timor filialis
Fears grieving a Father. Motive: love and awe of his character. Status: perfected by love. Ends in wisdom, intimacy, integrity.
Horizontal
timor humanus
Fears circumstances and people — lack, illness, opinion. Status: displaced as you rest in his care. Ends in cowardice if it rules.
Vertical
timor divinus
Recognizes his power and sovereignty. Motive: awe of the King's majesty. Status: cultivated. Ends in unshakable confidence.

Read the columns and the puzzle dissolves. Perfect love casts out fear is about the first one. The beginning of wisdom is about the second and fourth. And the third explains 2 Tim 1:7 and Isa 8:13 — when vertical fear rises, horizontal fear has nowhere to stand.

Interpretation

Whole-being worship: total attention — trembling and joyful at once — given to God as he actually is.

It keeps what every view above got right and loses nothing the texts require: it trembles (Ps 2:11), it obeys (Prov 8:13), it delights (Isa 11:3), it displaces every other fear (2 Tim 1:7), and it starts wisdom (Prov 9:10) — because wisdom begins the moment you finally see things at true size.

14Pulling the threads

Four threads that hold the whole thing together

Interpretation

01 · One capacity, two directions. Fear is total attention. Pointed at a threat, it's dread; pointed at worth, it's worship. Same vastness, two appraisals — that's the whole distance between the two fears of Ex 20:20, and it shows up in the body as two different nervous systems. It's also the ground under Jesus' word-swap in Matt 4:10.

02 · Punishment leaves. Awe stays. Servile fear dreads what God will do to you; filial fear dreads grieving him. The cross evicts the first (1 Jn 4:18); the covenant plants the second (Jer 32:40). Forgiveness doesn't end the fear of the Lord — it's where it finally begins (Ps 130:4).

03 · Wisdom is seeing true size. The fear of the Lord is reality at correct scale — God as big as he actually is, everything else where it actually sits. Awe researchers define their subject almost identically: vastness, plus a model that has to resize to fit it. Every wise decision downstream depends on that scale. That's why it's the beginning of wisdom (Prov 9:10), and why it ends in rest (Prov 19:23).

04 · One fear eats the rest — and only one can. All fear is a power-assessment: you fear what you believe can take something you love. Fear of the Lord recognizes he holds all of it — and that he's for you. Every other power gets demoted, not deleted; they can still act, they can't reach what he's holding. "Do not fear what they fear" (Isa 8:13). The person who trembles before God stops trembling before everything else (2 Tim 1:7), has no fear of bad news (Ps 112:7), and a church like that grows (Acts 9:31). It's the only fear that makes you fearless — because it's the only fear whose object loves you.
